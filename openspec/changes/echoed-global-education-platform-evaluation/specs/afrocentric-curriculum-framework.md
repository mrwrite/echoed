# afrocentric-curriculum-framework Specification

## Purpose
Define the long-term curriculum philosophy, authenticity standards, and cultural education framework that allow EchoEd to deliver academically credible, culturally authoritative, and globally inclusive education for African-American learners, African diaspora learners, African learners globally, and multicultural learners studying African and African-American history and culture.

This specification preserves the current lesson quality system, source-backed lessons, governance and review system, and course, unit, and lesson structure as the canonical curriculum foundation. Future curriculum maturity SHALL extend those systems rather than introducing duplicate curriculum systems.

## Requirements

### Requirement: Curriculum authenticity is grounded in historical accuracy and responsible scholarship
The curriculum framework SHALL require historically accurate, source-backed, and academically defensible instruction across all EchoEd curriculum intended for learner delivery.

#### Scenario: Lesson presents a historical topic
- **WHEN** a lesson teaches African, African-American, or diaspora history, culture, thought, or contribution
- **THEN** the lesson content is expected to reflect historically responsible interpretation grounded in credible sources rather than unsupported claims or superficial cultural framing

#### Scenario: Curriculum is prepared for publication
- **WHEN** curriculum is reviewed for learner-facing delivery
- **THEN** authenticity expectations are evaluated through the existing lesson quality and governance foundations rather than through a separate authenticity-only workflow disconnected from current review systems

### Requirement: Curriculum incorporates multiple perspectives without losing scholarly coherence
The curriculum framework SHALL support multiple perspectives, regional contexts, and interpretive depth while maintaining academic clarity and responsible educational synthesis.

#### Scenario: Topic includes contested or complex historical interpretation
- **WHEN** a lesson addresses a topic with multiple scholarly or historical perspectives
- **THEN** the curriculum presents that complexity responsibly, avoids false balance, and preserves age-appropriate instructional coherence

#### Scenario: Diaspora experience spans multiple regions
- **WHEN** a curriculum sequence covers African diaspora experiences across different geographies or traditions
- **THEN** the curriculum reflects regional distinction and historical context without collapsing diverse communities into a single undifferentiated narrative

### Requirement: Scholar, historian, educator, and cultural-institution involvement is a long-term curriculum expectation
The curriculum framework SHALL support long-term involvement from historians, educators, scholars, museums, archives, and other cultural institutions as part of curriculum authority and quality development.

#### Scenario: Curriculum requires deeper subject-matter authority
- **WHEN** a course, pathway, or lesson series addresses complex historical, philosophical, scientific, artistic, or political content
- **THEN** the framework expects review, guidance, attribution, or contribution from appropriately qualified experts over time

#### Scenario: Museum or archive-backed learning materials are used
- **WHEN** the curriculum incorporates museum, archive, or collection-based materials
- **THEN** those materials are treated as part of the source-backed curriculum model and remain connected to canonical lesson and course structures

### Requirement: Curriculum storytelling is culturally responsible and educationally serious
The curriculum framework SHALL require storytelling that is culturally responsible, identity-aware, and instructionally serious rather than performative, reductive, or merely inspirational.

#### Scenario: Lesson uses narrative storytelling
- **WHEN** a lesson teaches through story, biography, lived experience, or cultural narrative
- **THEN** the storytelling supports academic understanding, human dignity, and contextual accuracy

#### Scenario: Historically sensitive material is taught
- **WHEN** curriculum addresses oppression, displacement, enslavement, violence, or cultural harm
- **THEN** the framework requires historically honest treatment that preserves learner dignity and avoids sensationalism

### Requirement: Afrocentric educational philosophy centers African and diaspora contributions across disciplines
The curriculum framework SHALL define Afrocentric education as a broad intellectual orientation that recognizes African and diaspora contributions across history, science, mathematics, arts, literature, politics, economics, philosophy, culture, and global influence.

#### Scenario: Course is built within the Afrocentric framework
- **WHEN** curriculum is designed for EchoEd under this framework
- **THEN** it is expected to recognize African and diaspora presence as intellectually and historically central where appropriate rather than narrowly supplemental

#### Scenario: Learner studies a non-history topic
- **WHEN** a learner engages with science, arts, economics, civics, philosophy, or innovation-related content
- **THEN** the framework allows African and diaspora contributions to be meaningfully represented within the discipline rather than limiting Afrocentric study to heritage-only units

### Requirement: Identity-centered learning is academically grounded
The curriculum framework SHALL support identity-centered learning that strengthens learner belonging, dignity, and cultural literacy while preserving rigorous academic standards.

#### Scenario: African-American learner engages identity-relevant content
- **WHEN** a learner encounters curriculum connected to identity, heritage, or lived cultural context
- **THEN** the lesson affirms significance and belonging while remaining instructionally rigorous and source-aware

#### Scenario: Multicultural learner studies Afrocentric curriculum
- **WHEN** a learner outside the directly represented identity community studies the curriculum
- **THEN** the instruction remains globally legible, historically serious, and accessible without diluting Afrocentric authority

### Requirement: Curriculum scope spans early learning through lifelong learning
The curriculum framework SHALL support coherent scope across K-5, middle school, high school, GED preparation, college-prep pathways, and lifelong learning.

#### Scenario: Same theme is taught across age bands
- **WHEN** a major historical, cultural, or intellectual theme appears in more than one educational stage
- **THEN** the framework supports age-appropriate progression in complexity rather than isolated one-off treatment

#### Scenario: Adult or lifelong learner uses EchoEd
- **WHEN** a learner engages curriculum outside traditional K-12 sequencing
- **THEN** the curriculum remains academically serious and pathway-compatible while allowing adult-appropriate framing and pacing

### Requirement: African-American history extends beyond slavery and civil rights alone
The curriculum framework SHALL require African-American history to be taught as a broad, ongoing historical and intellectual tradition rather than being reduced only to slavery, segregation, or civil rights movements.

#### Scenario: Curriculum covers African-American history
- **WHEN** course or lesson sequences address African-American history
- **THEN** they include broader dimensions such as innovation, institution-building, philosophy, culture, arts, politics, economics, science, and global influence where educationally appropriate

#### Scenario: Learner studies modern Black life and influence
- **WHEN** curriculum addresses contemporary periods
- **THEN** it connects present-day achievement, challenge, creativity, and civic life to longer historical continuities

### Requirement: Global African history and diaspora history are treated as integral, not peripheral
The curriculum framework SHALL treat African history and African diaspora history as globally significant and interconnected areas of study.

#### Scenario: Curriculum addresses global African history
- **WHEN** a learner studies precolonial, colonial, modern, or contemporary African topics
- **THEN** the curriculum presents African societies, thought, innovation, governance, trade, culture, and global interaction with appropriate historical scope

#### Scenario: Curriculum connects Africa and diaspora
- **WHEN** a lesson sequence explores diasporic identity, exchange, migration, or influence
- **THEN** the curriculum connects regional stories without erasing local specificity

### Requirement: Instructional standards remain classroom-equivalent and age-appropriate
The curriculum framework SHALL require classroom-equivalent rigor, age appropriateness, and instructional design coherence across all supported educational levels.

#### Scenario: Lesson is written for younger learners
- **WHEN** curriculum is designed for K-5 or other younger age bands
- **THEN** the lesson preserves accuracy and cultural seriousness while adapting vocabulary, complexity, pacing, and activity design appropriately

#### Scenario: Lesson is written for secondary or college-prep learners
- **WHEN** curriculum is intended for older learners
- **THEN** the lesson supports deeper analysis, evidence use, discussion, and synthesis consistent with its level

### Requirement: Instruction supports discussion-driven, project-based, and literacy-rich learning
The curriculum framework SHALL support discussion-driven learning, project-based learning, literacy integration, and media-rich instruction through extensions of the current lesson quality model.

#### Scenario: Lesson includes discussion-based pedagogy
- **WHEN** a lesson is intended to build dialogue, reflection, or interpretation
- **THEN** the framework expects discussion prompts or equivalent structures to be represented through the existing lesson-quality foundation

#### Scenario: Lesson includes project or media-rich learning
- **WHEN** a lesson uses projects, media, archives, story, audio, or visual materials
- **THEN** those elements remain part of the canonical lesson and activity structure rather than a separate enrichment-only curriculum model

### Requirement: Assessment alignment is integral to curriculum design
The curriculum framework SHALL require that instructional goals, lesson flow, and learner evaluation remain aligned across the curriculum.

#### Scenario: Lesson declares learning objectives
- **WHEN** a lesson defines objectives or key concepts
- **THEN** the instructional activities and assessment or reflection components are expected to align with those objectives

#### Scenario: Pathway expects cumulative mastery
- **WHEN** a course or pathway is intended to produce measurable readiness or mastery
- **THEN** the curriculum framework expects assessments to reflect the stated academic outcomes

### Requirement: Source standards define acceptable evidence for curriculum authority
The curriculum framework SHALL define source expectations that include primary sources, scholarly sources, museums and archives, educational publishers, oral histories where appropriate, and clear source traceability.

#### Scenario: Lesson uses primary or archival evidence
- **WHEN** a lesson is supported by primary sources, museum collections, archival artifacts, or other historically significant material
- **THEN** the lesson keeps those references traceable through the existing source-backed lesson model

#### Scenario: Lesson uses oral history
- **WHEN** oral history or community testimony is educationally appropriate
- **THEN** it is used with contextual care, citation discipline, and clarity about the kind of evidence being presented

### Requirement: Citations and source traceability remain part of canonical lesson quality
The curriculum framework SHALL preserve citations and source traceability as part of the canonical lesson quality and readiness expectations.

#### Scenario: Learner-facing curriculum cites evidence
- **WHEN** curriculum references historical claims, quotations, narratives, or factual assertions
- **THEN** the lesson maintains traceable citation information through the current source-backed lesson structure

#### Scenario: Reviewer evaluates lesson credibility
- **WHEN** a reviewer inspects a lesson for approval readiness
- **THEN** source presence, citation quality, and traceability remain part of the normal governance and readiness evaluation process

### Requirement: Inclusivity reflects global African diversity and learner diversity
The curriculum framework SHALL require inclusive representation across African, African-American, and broader diaspora experiences while remaining educationally coherent for diverse learner communities.

#### Scenario: Curriculum represents multiple diaspora communities
- **WHEN** a course or unit addresses Black life across regions, eras, or traditions
- **THEN** the curriculum includes meaningful diversity of geography, language context, migration history, and cultural expression where appropriate

#### Scenario: Curriculum is used by diverse learner populations
- **WHEN** learners with different identities, prior knowledge, and cultural relationships to the topic use EchoEd
- **THEN** the framework supports clarity, dignity, and inclusive access without weakening Afrocentric authority

### Requirement: Multilingual, accessibility, and varied-environment considerations are part of curriculum design
The curriculum framework SHALL support multilingual considerations, accessibility expectations, and varied learning environments including classrooms, homeschool settings, and globally distributed access conditions.

#### Scenario: Curriculum is delivered in a constrained learning environment
- **WHEN** a learner accesses EchoEd in a low-bandwidth, mobile-first, or nontraditional educational context
- **THEN** the curriculum remains understandable, accessible, and structurally coherent within the canonical course and lesson system

#### Scenario: Curriculum must serve diverse language contexts
- **WHEN** future curriculum expansion requires multilingual or regionally adaptive presentation
- **THEN** the framework treats linguistic accessibility as an extension of the canonical curriculum model rather than a separate alternate curriculum track

### Requirement: Governance alignment preserves one canonical curriculum quality workflow
The curriculum framework SHALL align with the existing governance and review system and SHALL extend it to reflect educator, scholar, and curriculum-authority expectations over time.

#### Scenario: Lesson is submitted for approval
- **WHEN** a lesson progresses through the current review workflow
- **THEN** approval expectations include curriculum quality, source integrity, instructional coherence, and cultural responsibility within the same governance logic family

#### Scenario: Curriculum requires specialist review
- **WHEN** a lesson, unit, or course needs scholar, historian, educator, or cultural review input
- **THEN** the framework expects that review to extend the current governance model rather than creating a detached specialist-only curriculum system

### Requirement: Ongoing curriculum revision is expected as part of cultural and academic responsibility
The curriculum framework SHALL treat curriculum revision, refinement, and re-review as normal parts of maintaining long-term educational credibility.

#### Scenario: New scholarship or improved sourcing becomes available
- **WHEN** curriculum content can be strengthened by updated research, better evidence, or improved interpretive framing
- **THEN** the framework expects revision through the existing curriculum and governance structures

#### Scenario: Curriculum requires periodic quality review
- **WHEN** a published lesson, course, or pathway remains in active use over time
- **THEN** the framework supports periodic reassessment for accuracy, relevance, and cultural responsibility without replacing the canonical curriculum structure
